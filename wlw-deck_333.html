Your Baseline Practice — Do This Every Time You Leave the House

"Eye contact and a slow smile is not flirting. It's presence. Make it your resting state in public."

Step 01

Walking in: make eye contact with the first three people you pass. Hold it one beat longer than you normally would. Don't look away first.

Step 02

At any moment of stillness — waiting, browsing, watching — let your eyes be soft and open instead of on your phone.

Step 03

When someone looks at you and you feel it, don't look away immediately. Let there be a beat. Then decide what to do with it.

Step 04

Smile like you mean it when you make eye contact. Not a full grin — the slow, self-possessed kind. That's the move.

Goal

10 genuine eye contact + smile moments before you approach a single person. This is your warmup. Every time. Non-negotiable.

The Date Progression Track — How You Actually Get There

01

Baseline

Eye contact + smile at minimum 10 people. You're warming up your social nervous system, not hunting. No conversation required yet.

02

The Open

One intentional conversation with someone you find genuinely interesting. Use an observation, a question, or wave them over. Three minutes minimum.

03

The Return

Go back to someone you already spoke to earlier in the night. "I've been thinking about what you said about—" This is where things actually happen.

04

The Exchange

Give her a way to reach you — your number, email, or a digital business card. Keep it natural. You don’t have to make it a moment.

05

Making Your Interest Known

Before the night ends, say what you're open to — keep it light, give her options. Something like: "I'd be down for drinks, or pastries somewhere, or a smoothie — nothing complicated." You've shown interest. She can do the planning.

06

The Actual Date

You followed through on the soft pitch. You confirmed within 48 hours. You showed up. That's the card. That's the win.

🃏

The Coochie Card — Your Dating Business Card

Keep a small stack. Hand one only when you genuinely felt something — not as a polite exit, not to collect a follower. It says: I chose you on purpose. The card's power is its scarcity. If you hand it out to everyone, it means nothing. If you hand it out to her, it means everything.

How to open the space — your menu of moves

Observation

Say something true about the room, the art, the moment. Not about them yet. Let them respond before you introduce yourself.

Compliment

Be specific. Not "cute fit." Describe the piece, the styling choice, the detail only someone paying attention would notice.

Question

Ask something you're genuinely curious about. Genuine is always more attractive than clever.

Wave Over

Eye contact, slight head tilt, slow half-smile. Let them come to you. Then you speak first once they arrive.

Hi + Eye Contact

Full eye contact, warm smile, one word. Then let silence breathe for two beats. They'll fill it. That's the moment.

Spread the News

"You have to see the piece in the next room." Give them something worth having before you introduce yourself.

Sprinkle Value

Share a fact, a recommendation, insider intel. You're a resource, not a petitioner.

Random Kindness

Hold a door, offer a light, grab a napkin. Let the conversation open naturally after. Don't explain the gesture.

Put in a Request

"Can you hold this for a second?" Small ask. Big moment. Creates an opening with zero pressure.

Casually Cut In

Join a group conversation naturally. Add something that moves it forward. Don't announce yourself — just arrive.

Change It Up

Walk over with no plan. Let your presence speak first. This is for when you're feeling yourself and you know it.

Friend-Making — What You're Actually Looking For

The Go-Getter

Asks about your work with real curiosity. Talks about what she's building. Ambitious without competing with yours.

The Social Dynamo

The main character who makes rooms feel alive. Find her, tell her she's good at it, and let her pull you into her orbit.

The Host Friend

She organizes things. Show up to what she creates. Bring something. Say what was good. She'll invite you back.

The Show-Up Friend

Answers when it's real. Remembers what you mentioned offhand. Shows up without being asked. You need two. They reveal themselves over time.

Unmasked ND Energy

Hyperfocused, direct, genuinely themselves. Find what they're lit up about and ask about it specifically. Don't redirect.

Friend vs Date Routing

Do you want to know about her life, or be in it? Comfortable near her = friend. Pulled toward her = date. Name it to yourself.

Dating — Tone, Pace & Standards

Setting the Tone

You set the tone by being fully yourself — warm, present, unhurried, unbothered by outcome. People calibrate to your energy. Arrive grounded and enjoying yourself. Your presence is the standard. (Anwar White: the woman who knows her worth doesn't chase — she receives.)

The Rung Architecture

Not everyone gets the same access. Strangers get warmth. Acquaintances get your time at events. Dates get a call and a low-frills first meet. Partners get consistency and trust — earned over time. No one skips rungs. No one creeps up without demonstrating the behavior that earns the next level.

Emotional Vetting

Watch for: do they regulate their emotions or export them to you? Do they initiate repairs or disappear? Are they consistent — same energy texting, calling, in person — or do they shape-shift? Inconsistency is data. Collect it quietly before investing more.

Intentionality Vetting

Intentional people make plans and follow through. They don't leave you in ambiguity because ambiguity serves them. Ask yourself after every interaction: did this feel clear or did I fill in blanks? Clarity is care. Vagueness is a boundary violation in slow motion.

The Ask Test

Ask for something small early — a recommendation, an opinion, a small favor. People who show up for a small ask can show up when it matters. People who deflect or disappear on low-stakes requests will do the same later. Ask early. Watch what happens.

Pace — Let Them Pursue

You show interest — warmth, presence, a couple of low-key options. Then you wait. The pursuit tells you everything about their capacity to invest. Someone who doesn't pursue when given an opening is either not interested or not ready. Don't fill the silence.

Core 5 — One Rung for Your Partner

Your Core 5 is your most intimate circle. One slot is ideally held for your future partner. Core 5 requires shared values — not just shared vibe. How they treat people with less power, how they hold their word, how they love, and what they're building.

Non-Negotiables

They plan and pursue. They are emotionally regulated and available. They celebrate you without competing. Consistent — not intense then absent. They make you feel free, not managed. They have their own purpose and capacity to give. You don't shrink, explain, or perform.

Friendship — Dunbar's Circles + Reciprocity

Core 5 — Daily or Weekly

Chosen family. Shared values (not just interests), mutual showing up, emotional safety, willingness to go out of your way for each other. To earn this rung: showed up when it cost them something, held what you shared with care, demonstrated values alignment over time. One slot ideally for your future partner.

Close 15 — Every Couple of Weeks

Real friends. Shared values broadly, genuine curiosity about each other's lives, reciprocal effort, some vulnerability held with care. The jump from acquaintance requires one real conversation where something true was said and received well. Danielle Bayard Jackson: healthy friendships have a rhythm of mutual reaching — bid and response.

Affectionate 50 — Monthly

People you're genuinely glad to see. Warm acquaintances, event friends, fellow regulars. This circle doesn't require maintenance — just warmth when paths cross. A warm acquaintance who stays warm is more valuable than a forced close friendship. Don't overpromise here.

The Rung Architecture

People stay at the rung they've demonstrated. Someone who only reaches out when they need something stays at 50, not 15. Someone present but not values-aligned stays at 15, not 5. The rung is accurate, not punitive. You calibrate access based on behavior, not potential.

Going Out of Your Way

Real friendship requires inconvenience sometimes — showing up to something that matters to them, remembering and following up, initiating when they haven't. This is the tax of intimacy. It tells you who belongs at which rung: who goes out of their way for you?

The Ask Test + Reciprocity Check

Ask something small early and watch who responds. At 6 weeks, check the temperature without keeping score. Danielle Bayard Jackson's bid-and-response: are bids for connection being met? Nedra Tawwab (Set Boundaries, Find Peace): if you're over-functioning, pull back slightly and observe what happens next.

Networking — Entertainment Industry

Freelance Film + Photo

Your next job comes from who remembers you warmly and trusts your work. Myleik Teele: earned access — you don't get the room by asking for it, you get it by being someone people want in the room. Be excellent, easy to work with, follow through without being followed up on.

Day Job / Agency Track

Studios and agencies hire people they've heard of positively or who come recommended. Jodie Taylor: strategic visibility — be in the rooms before you have the credits to get in on merit. Junior boards, ERG events, and industry panels are how you build a name before a reel does it.

Know Your Worth Before the Room

Emma Grede: know your value before you walk in, because the room will not assign it to you. Carry it in. Know your one-line: what you make, what you're working on, what you're building. Not a pitch — a presence. The most magnetic people know exactly who they are.

Cultural Authority

Bozoma St. John: be unforgettable by being fully yourself — your Blackness, your queerness, your creative identity are not variables to manage, they are what makes you irreplaceable. The goal isn't to fit into the room — it's to be who the room remembers.

Visibility → Credibility → Trust → Opportunity

You can't skip stages. Visibility: they know your name. Credibility: they know you're good. Trust: they'd put their name behind you. Opportunity: they think of you when something opens. Each stage is built through consistent behavior over time, not one impressive interaction.

Making Asks That Land

Lead with value before any ask. Be specific — vague asks are easy to defer. Make it easy to say yes: 20-minute call, one intro, one piece of advice. Don't follow up more than twice. Myleik Teele: the ask should feel consistent with every interaction before it, not like a transaction that came out of nowhere.

Multi-Mode — When It's Everything at Once

Main Character Energy

The goal isn't to be strategic all night. It's to be so fully yourself — curious, warm, enjoying the room — that the right people find you. Main character energy is not performance. It's presence. You're not working the room by hitting every corner. You're working it by being the person everyone is a little more alive near.

Set Your Mode First

Decide your primary intention before you walk in — not exclusively, just primarily. "Tonight I'm here to connect, dating is a bonus" feels different in your body than "tonight I'm looking." Your energy broadcasts your intention. Walking in with clarity makes everything easier and more enjoyable.

Reading Your Mode

Networking: curious about what they do and where they're going. Friendship: curious about who they are and how they think. Dating: you feel something — a pull, a warmth, a wanting to stay past the point of usefulness. These can overlap. Name what you're feeling so you can act on it clearly.

The 15-Minute Rule

Cap romantic and networking conversations at 15 minutes unless something extraordinary is happening. End it while it's good. Friendship conversations are the exception — those can go as long as they want. A clean exit: "This was actually really good — I'm going to go find my people." Warm. Final. No awkwardness.

Pivoting Between Modes

You can pivot mid-conversation. Networking to dating: slow down, get warmer, stop talking about work. Dating to friendship: relax the undertone, widen the conversation. The pivot is internal — you don't announce it. Your energy shifts and they'll feel it.

Protect Your Energy

Multi-mode nights are the most draining. Know your limit. After a certain point, shift to enjoyment-only mode — stop networking, stop looking, just be in the room with no agenda. The best connections often happen when you've stopped trying. Fun is also a strategy. Give yourself permission to just have a good time.
